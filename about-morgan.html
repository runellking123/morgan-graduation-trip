Biography

A Life In Education

Let me tell you about a woman who did not wait for permission.

She came up in Baytown, Texas — not the kind of town that makes the evening news, but the kind that makes people. The kind where front porches still mean something, where your neighbor knows your mama’s name, and where children learn early that love is not soft. It is structured. It is deliberate. It is the thing that holds you accountable long before the world ever will. Marvin and Lilly Bennett raised their daughter that way — with tenderness in one hand and expectation in the other — and Morgan received both as the gifts they were.

She walked out of Ross S. Sterling High School the way Black women have always walked out of the places that shaped them: with something burning behind her eyes that nobody gave her and nobody could take.

At Lamar University, she earned a Bachelor of Science in Health Science, studying the body and its systems, how people break down and how they are restored. But healing, she discovered, was not only clinical. It was relational. It happened in rooms where someone looked a child in the face and said, I see you. I believe you can. That is what pulled Morgan into the classroom. Not a career plan. A calling.

She taught elementary and middle school in Houston. Then New Orleans — a city that knows something about rebuilding. Then Baton Rouge. Then White Castle, Louisiana, a small town where the work was no smaller. She moved through these communities the way water moves through soil — quietly, deliberately, nourishing everything it touches. She was not performing education. She was practicing it, the way her parents practiced love: daily, without fanfare, without rest.

And because she was her mother’s daughter, she went back to school. She earned a Master of Education in Curriculum and Instruction from Grand Canyon University. Then an Educational Specialist degree from the same institution. Two graduate degrees on top of a career already in motion. Understand — this is not ambition for ambition’s sake. This is a woman who respects the craft enough to study it from the inside out, who refuses to stand in front of a classroom and not know why what she’s doing works.

She moved into secondary education administration in Baton Rouge, where the scope of her influence widened from a single classroom to an entire building. Then she stepped into higher education as Assistant Dean of Education at St. Augustine University in Raleigh, North Carolina — an HBCU, a place that understood her before she had to explain herself. There, she mentored faculty, shaped academic programs, and carried forward a tradition as old as the institutions themselves: the belief that education is not a commodity. It is a covenant.

Today, Dr. Morgan King serves as Assistant Provost at Wiley University in Marshall, Texas, where she oversees academic departments and faculty affairs. She sits at the table where decisions are made about curriculum, about standards, about what students will carry with them when they leave. It is not a small seat. She earned it.

And on June 27, 2026, she will stand inside the Broward County Convention Center in Fort Lauderdale, Florida, surrounded by the people who held her up, and she will receive her Doctor of Education from Capella University. Let that sit with you for a moment. Four degrees. B.S. M.Ed. Ed.S. Ed.D. Each one earned in the margins of a life that was already full — between grading papers and giving baths, between faculty meetings and bedtime stories, between the woman the world needed her to be and the woman she was still becoming.

She is a proud member of Alpha Kappa Alpha Sorority, Incorporated — a sisterhood built on service, scholarship, and the understanding that when one woman rises, she is obligated to reach back. She is married to Dr. Runell J. King, and together they are raising two sons — Kai, age five, and Khol, age two — who give her every reason to keep building and absolutely no reason to sleep. She cooks with intention. She travels with abandon — point her iPhone in a direction and she will go. And she stays close to the people who made her through daily FaceTime calls with her mother and her brother-in-law, Dr. Jovan T. Thomas, because Morgan King understands something that degrees cannot teach you: no matter how far you go, you must never get too far from where you started.

That is who she is. Educator. Scholar. Mother. Leader. Daughter of Baytown. Woman of purpose. And now — Doctor.

Four degrees. Five cities. Two sons watching. One woman who never stopped.
